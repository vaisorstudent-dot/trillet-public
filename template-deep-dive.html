The Problem

Hand-built agents
don't scale.

Every new client should not require a fresh build. But today, that is how most agency voice deployments work.

The same voice settings, integrations, call logic, and business data get rebuilt again and again. At small scale this is annoying. At 10+ clients it becomes operational drag. At 30+ it becomes the ceiling.

Template architecture is what turns agency delivery from repeated labor into reusable product.

Without templates, the agency ladder has a growth ceiling. With templates, Scale ($497) becomes the natural destination.

The Object Hierarchy

Five objects, not one.

The market does not disagree about whether templates matter. It disagrees, implicitly, about what a template actually is.

1 Prompt Preset Just language. A reusable system prompt. No config, no integrations.
2 Agent Blueprint Full configured agent. Prompt + voice + tools + KB + call settings. Duplicated as-is.
3 Template Parameterized blueprint. Variables are explicit, not implicit. Designed to be instantiated, not just copied.
4 Deployment Pack Template + integrations + onboarding form + defaults. Everything needed to go from "I want this" to "working agent."
5 Marketplace Asset Pack + listing + pricing + IP protection + versioning. A distributable commercial object.

The product ladder should not gate "more template features." It should gate movement up this object hierarchy.

Worked Example: Dental Receptionist

One vertical. Five objects.

Prompt Preset
"You are a dental office receptionist. Greet callers warmly, answer questions about hours, services, and insurance. Book appointments when requested."
Just text. No voice, no tools, no structure.
↓
Agent Blueprint
Prompt + warm female voice + calendar booking (Cal.com) + transfer to office manager + KB: insurance plans, services menu, hours + call settings (voicemail detection, 3-min max). A working agent for one specific practice.
↓
Template
Same configured agent, but key business inputs are now structured variables: {{business_name}}, {{hours}}, {{services}}, {{insurance_providers}}, {{transfer_number}}. Those inputs are collected through a form. The agency is no longer copying an agent and manually editing it. It is instantiating a reusable object.
Worked Example: Dental (continued)
Deployment Pack
A deployment pack is not just the template. It is the template plus the path to deployment: default phone setup, calendar binding, CRM connection pattern, voice default, onboarding form, launch checklist. Everything from "I want a dental receptionist" to working agent taking calls.
↓
Marketplace Asset
A marketplace asset is a deployment pack made commercial. "Dental Practice Pack" -- listing, pricing ($197 one-time), versioning (v2.1 adds insurance verification), preview, and controlled distribution. Agency that built it earns on every installation. Horizon 2 -- not now.

Prompts are reusable language. Deployment packs are reusable delivery systems. The gap between them is the template architecture.

Horizon 1: What We Build Now

Horizon 1 is not marketplace.

It is parameterize, collect inputs, instantiate.

1
Parameterization Engine
Turn hard-coded business values into structured variables.
2
Input Surface
A form that collects those values cleanly from the user.
3
Instantiation Path
Generate and deploy a working agent from the completed input set.

The H1 question is simple: can Trillet move from copied agents to instantiated agents?

For Ming and Laurence

Four questions to scope Horizon 1.

1
What exactly is the agent object today? What are its atomic components and dependencies?
2
Can a configured agent be duplicated cleanly across workspaces today? Not conceptually. Actually.
3
Where can variables live? Prompt only, or deeper in the config object?
4
What is the real deployment path from filled form to live agent? What already exists, what is missing, what is hard?

These are engineering feasibility questions, not strategy questions. The answers determine how much of H1 is real platform capability and how much requires new build.

Commercial Logic

How template architecture creates value.

1
Upgrade pressure
Templates make Scale ($497) irresistible, not aspirational. Agencies that create templates need markup, full API, and distribution -- all Scale gates.
2
Deployment velocity
Faster client onboarding = more clients = more usage. A 3-minute intake form replaces a 45-minute build session.
3
Usage expansion
More agents deployed per agency, more per-minute charges flowing. Templates lower the cost of adding the next client to near zero.
Most near-term value is not pack sales. It is upgrades plus more live agents.

Activation is the master variable. If templates do not materially increase deployed and active agents, the commercial case weakens fast. If they do, every revenue line gets stronger.

Horizon 2: Explicitly Deferred

Distribution, not yet exchange.

Versioning, cross-agency distribution, paid marketplace, take rates. All architecturally correct. All premature before four conditions are met:

01

500+ active agencies on the platform. Distribution needs volume.

02

50+ Scale-tier agencies creating templates. Suppliers must exist.

03

40%+ activation rate proven. Templates must actually get deployed.

04

Pack format mature. The deployment pack shape must stabilize before distribution.

Until these conditions are true, Trillet should think in terms of first-party and approved packs, not marketplace economics.

Open question: voice agent IP may be thinner than GHL snapshot IP (4-8 hours to build vs. hundreds of hours). Whether there is enough value density for marketplace demand is unproven.

Template architecture is what makes the agency ladder operationally real.

Clarify the object system. Build Horizon 1. Prove activation. Earn Horizon 2.

The four questions are the next step.
