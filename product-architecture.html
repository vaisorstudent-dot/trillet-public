Trillet Agency Product Architecture

Two-layer architecture brief. The top layer is a category read: what market we're in, what buyers are being taught to expect, and where the category is still weak. The bottom layer is the product consequence: how the agency ladder and template object system should be structured given that read. Strategy first -- current product is input, not ceiling. Grounded in competitive research (6 competitors + GHL, cross-validated against GPT-5.4 and Perplexity), template anatomy research (7 competitors across 3 axes), five-object ontology, and commercial model v2.

The Category

Agency white-label voice AI is an emerging category, not a mature one. There is no dominant player, no settled buyer expectation, and no standard product shape. The market has 15-20+ active companies, most launched in the last 18 months. Buyers are forming their mental model of what this product category is right now, based on whichever platforms they encounter first.

The category splits along two axes that matter:

AxisCamp ACamp BWhy It Matters
Architecture Full-stackTrillet, Insighto, EchoWin, Assistable WrapperVoiceAIWrapper, ChatDash, Stammer Full-stack owns the voice infrastructure: lower COGS, quality control, single failure point. Wrappers stack provider markup plus platform margin, have no quality control, and inherit cascading provider dependencies. Structurally deficient on every operational axis.
Billing model Markup-basedUniversal. Agency sets retail price, keeps spread. Platform monetizes via subscription + per-minute. Not a split axis. Every competitor (including Trillet) uses markup-based billing. The real differentiation is degree of pricing autonomy per tier: pass-through at lower tiers, full markup at mid-tier, full SaaS-mode plan design at top tier. Agencies expect to own their pricing from the first real agency tier.

The category is converging toward a recognizable shape: a tiered agency ladder where gates are commercial capability (sub-accounts, billing power, API scope), not product features. Every competitor uses some version of this. The convergence point is around $300-500/mo for full agency capability. Below that is testing; above is SaaS-mode autonomy.

What Competitors Are Teaching Agencies to Expect

This is more important than what competitors have. The expectations being set right now define the category floor. An agency shopping this space in 2026 is learning to expect:

FACT Category expectations being formed

  1. Usage markup from the first real agency tier. Agencies expect to set their own per-minute rate to clients and keep the spread. Agency profit = subscription margin + usage margin. Without usage margin, the unit economics don't work for them.
  2. White-label from the first real tier. Custom domain, branding, "Powered by" removal. Not a premium add-on.
  3. Sub-account management as the scaling unit. Agencies think in client accounts, not agent counts.
  4. Full commercial autonomy at the top self-serve tier (~$500/mo). At the highest agency tier, agencies expect to create their own subscription plans, set their own pricing, and have clients sign up and pay without manual provisioning. Multiple competitors gate this at $497-499. The tier ladder is a pricing-autonomy ladder: usage markup at the mid-tier, full plan design and automated client billing at the top.
  5. Concurrency as a visible metric. Not hidden. Marketed per tier. Agencies are being trained to ask about this. Trillet currently offers unlimited concurrency across all tiers -- a structural advantage from full-stack infrastructure that competitors cannot match.
  6. BYOK / multi-provider options. Agencies shopping multiple platforms are encountering BYO-key support (Assistable, Insighto, VoiceAIWrapper). High-volume agencies expect to plug in their own AI provider keys for cost control. Becoming a category norm at Scale-tier equivalents.

Competitive Pressure Points

Where the competitive environment is creating urgency for Trillet to act. The market has moved. These are real products, not paper tigers.

INFERENCE Pressure sources (re-crawled Mar 11, 2026)

  1. Template systems are approaching from multiple directions, and the window is closing. ChatDash V2 has agent templates (still provider-constrained to Retell/OpenAI). Stammer has a chat marketplace. GHL has snapshots with paid distribution. VoiceAIWrapper has Products & Packs as a packaging mechanic. Nobody has combined all three axes (completeness + parameterization + distribution) for voice AI yet -- but pieces are being assembled. The structural opportunity remains: the marketplace model and the voice template model exist in different products. But competitors are building toward convergence.
  2. Wrapper economics are compressing mid-tier value. VoiceAIWrapper at $29/mo and ChatDash V2 at $120/mo make "good enough" white-label voice AI very cheap. Agencies who don't need full-stack quality control can get branded portals with Stripe billing for a fraction of Trillet's price. This doesn't threaten Trillet's serious agencies (who need reliability), but it erodes the "try voice AI" entry market.
  3. Concurrency is becoming a marketed differentiator. Insighto now offers 50 concurrent calls at $299 and 100 at $499. Assistable restructured its entire tier system around concurrency (5/10/15/20 by tier). The market is starting to understand that concurrency is the infrastructure quality signal. If Trillet doesn't actively market unlimited concurrency, others will claim the narrative with lower but published numbers.
  4. SaaS tooling has matured across the category. Stammer has a fully documented SaaS Configurator with 4 add-on types (agent rebilling, storage, MasterChat prompts, scraper). Assistable built an interactive pricing calculator with a rebilling multiplier (1–3x) showing real-time profit margins -- the most transparent cost UX in the market. VoiceAIWrapper's SaaS Creator automates client onboarding via website embed. Agencies can now comparison-shop billing infrastructure. The baseline has risen. (See Open Item 5 for detailed capability decomposition.)
  5. The category is becoming visible. Competitors are publishing comparison blogs, pricing breakdowns, and "best white-label" listicles (Insighto alone has 5+ SEO-targeted posts). ConvoCore.ai, a new entrant not in our Round 3 research, is positioning with "82% margins" and 4–6¢/min. Trillet's own blog (Ming) is contributing content here, which is positive. But the quiet-category advantage -- where Trillet could position without competition for attention -- is eroding.

Trillet High-Leverage Opportunities

Trillet's opportunity is not more features. It is being the first voice AI platform where adding clients does not make the agency's cost, fragility, or labor rise in lockstep. Every opportunity below maps through the product ladder and speaks in buyer outcomes, not product specs. Ordered by strategic leverage.

1

Trillet has unlimited concurrent calls at every tier today. But "unlimited concurrency" is an infrastructure spec. The buyer-facing meaning is more important: on Trillet, scaling your agency does not trigger hidden costs. Every competitor either gates concurrency for monetization (Stammer, Assistable, Insighto) or can't control it because they don't own the infrastructure (wrappers). Trillet can afford unlimited because full-stack COGS are structurally lower. The cost advantage expresses as a trust advantage.

2

Template Object System Category-shaping. Full architecture on the Template Architecture tab.

Competitors offer fragments: agent clones, prompt-only parameterization, or internal-only reuse. No voice AI platform combines artifact completeness, full-stack parameterization, and cross-agency commercial distribution. Trillet can define the category with a five-object hierarchy -- from deployment packs that let Entry agencies deploy in minutes, to marketplace assets that let Scale agencies sell templates to other agencies.

This is not just a differentiator. It is ladder logic (each tier unlocks a qualitatively different object: consume → create → distribute), product logic (the object system makes the 10th client a form fill, not a build), and moat logic (agency IP lock-in, distribution gravity, per-minute usage flywheel on every installation).

Commercially: ~$86K/yr at 250 agencies (base case), with 87-92% of value from subscription upgrade pressure. The master variable is activation rate -- whether template-deployed workspaces produce working agents, not empty shells. See Template Architecture →

The Three Buyer Transitions That Matter Most

CONSTRAINT Regime boundaries

  1. D2C → Starter: The buyer stops using Trillet for themselves and starts serving clients. This is the regime boundary. Starter gets the full core product: white-label, markup, API, templates.
  2. Starter → Agency: The buyer outgrows 5 workspaces. Same product, more capacity (10 workspaces + $20/ea overage). This is the scale gate.
  3. Agency → Scale: The buyer needs SaaS Configurator (automated client self-signup), custom agents, BYOK, the $0.10/min volume discount, or more than 10 workspaces at scale. This is the productization gate. 15 workspaces included + $20/ea overage.

Template Object System

This is not a feature. It is an object system that changes the agency ladder from consume to create to distribute. We are defining the right strategic architecture first, then deriving what product and engineering must build to make it real. Current product is input, not ceiling.

1. The Object Hierarchy

The word "template" conflates five distinct product objects. Each is more complete than the last. The tier ladder maps to this hierarchy -- each upgrade is a business model transition, not a feature unlock.

ObjectWhat it isWho uses itBusiness mode
Prompt Preset Reusable system prompt or prompt fragment. Just language. No agent config, no integrations, no parameterization UX. Anyone. Agency, platform, community. Copy-paste starting point. Same as what most competitors call a "template."
Agent Blueprint Complete configured agent snapshot: prompt, voice, KB structure, tool configs, call settings, speech settings. Duplicatable but not parameterized -- a raw copy, not a generalized form. Entry+ agencies duplicating a working agent for a similar client. INFERENCE Why this works for Trillet

  1. Sub-account cap is the natural upgrade trigger. Agencies hit 3 clients and upgrade to Core. They hit 10 and upgrade to Scale. No artificial feature gates needed. Growth forces the upgrade.
  2. Per-minute amplification. Every template installation creates a new metered workspace. A popular marketplace template with 200 installations = 200 workspaces of per-minute revenue Trillet didn't sell. GHL doesn't have this monetization layer.
  3. IP lock-in. Agencies who build template libraries on Trillet can't port them. Full creation access from Entry means they start building IP immediately, deepening lock-in from day one.

4. What Must Be Built

Current product is the input. The strategic architecture is right for the market. But not everything needs to be built at once. The engineering scope splits into two horizons with a clear dependency: Horizon 1 proves the object system works. Horizon 2 builds the exchange infrastructure around it. H2 should not begin until H1 is proven.

Horizon 1: Prove the Object System NOW

These three capabilities make objects 1-4 (preset, blueprint, template, deployment pack) real. They are the engineering conversation for Ming.

CapabilityWhat it doesWhy it matters
Parameterization engine Structured variable fields across the full agent config: prompt, voice model, KB structure, integration bindings, call settings, speech behavior. The key differentiator. ChatDash only parameterizes prompt text. GHL parameterizes full sub-accounts but isn't voice AI. Full-stack parameterization in voice AI does not exist. This is the hardest engineering question and the first one to scope.
Intake form builder Agency defines which variables are exposed to the deployer. Field types, defaults, validation rules, config mapping. The form IS the template product. Without it, "template" is just another word for "agent clone." This is what separates object 3 (template) from object 2 (blueprint).
Template instantiation "Fill form → configured agent appears in workspace" with all bindings resolved. Single action. The deployment speed claim depends on this. Without instant instantiation, templates compress labor but don't compress time. This is what makes deployment packs (object 4) actually deployable.

If H1 works, Trillet has parameterized templates and deployment packs that agencies can use internally. That alone delivers the 87-92% of value (upgrade pressure from the object hierarchy) and proves the activation hypothesis (do template-deployed workspaces actually produce working agents?).

Horizon 2: Build the Exchange LATER

These three capabilities make object 5 (marketplace asset) and the marketplace system real. They should not be scoped until H1 is proven and the conditions below are met.

CONSTRAINT Conditions before H2 matters

  • Agency base reaches 500+ (enough buyer density for a real exchange)
  • Scale tier exists with 50+ agencies who have proven, maintained templates
  • Template-deployed workspaces show 40%+ activation (proving packs produce working agents, not empty shells)
  • Deployment pack format is mature enough that third-party packs reliably work (quality is provable, not just claimed)

Until those conditions, the "marketplace" is Trillet publishing first-party vertical packs and agencies consuming them. That is a curated catalog, not an exchange. The catalog is valuable (it proves the format, seeds adoption, and accelerates Entry agencies). But it does not require marketplace infrastructure.

6. Open Questions

The Ming/Laurence conversation should focus on Horizon 1: can we build parameterized templates and deployment packs? Object-by-object, starting from what exists today.

For the Ming conversation (Horizon 1 scope)

OPEN 1. What is our agent config object today?
When you create an agent, what is the full atomic config? Prompt, voice, KB, integrations, call settings, speech behavior -- what fields exist, what is structured vs freeform? This is the foundation. The parameterization engine can only parameterize what the config object exposes.
OPEN 2. Can an agent be duplicated across workspaces today?
What copies and what doesn't? If duplication exists, we have agent blueprints (object 2) as a product reality. If not, that's the first build.
OPEN 3. How hard is full-stack parameterization?
Adding structured variable fields across the full agent config -- not just prompt text, but voice model, KB structure, integration bindings, call settings. This is the hardest engineering question and the key differentiator. What would the technical path look like? Is the agent config structured enough to support this, or does it need restructuring first?
OPEN 4. What would "fill form, get working agent" require?
Template instantiation: the intake form is filled, all bindings resolve, a configured agent appears in the workspace ready to take calls. Is this a straightforward automation of existing config steps, or does it require new architecture? This is what makes deployment packs (object 4) truly deployable vs dressed-up clones.

For later (Horizon 2 scope)

H2 5. Cross-workspace distribution
What would it take to package an agent config for installation in another agency's account? What are the cross-workspace boundaries today? IP protection at the config level? This becomes relevant when Scale tier and marketplace are live, not before.
H2 6. Is voice agent IP deep enough for marketplace demand?
GHL snapshots capture hundreds of hours of workflow/funnel/CRM config. A Trillet voice agent template might take 4-8 hours to build well. Is that enough embedded expertise to create real marketplace demand, or does the marketplace only work if deployment packs include significant non-trivial content (tested conversation flows, edge case handling, integration configs, onboarding workflows)? The answer determines whether the marketplace is a real exchange or a thin catalog.

7. Marketplace System HORIZON 2

The marketplace asset is an object. The marketplace is a system -- the exchange infrastructure around the object. This section defines the target architecture for the exchange. It is strategically important to design now so the earlier horizons build toward it, but it should not be engineered until the conditions in section 4 are met.

CONSTRAINT Honest question: does the GHL parallel fully transfer?

GHL snapshots capture hundreds of hours of complex funnel, workflow, CRM, and automation config. That depth of embedded IP creates real marketplace demand -- agencies pay $97-$997 for a snapshot because rebuilding it would take weeks. A Trillet voice agent is structurally simpler: prompt + voice + KB + integrations + call settings. A well-built dental template might take 4-8 hours to create. Is that enough embedded IP to generate marketplace demand, or does the marketplace framing import GHL logic that doesn't fully transfer to voice agents? This is an open question, not a settled finding. The answer may be that the marketplace works only if deployment packs include significant non-trivial content (tested conversation flows, edge case handling, integration configs, onboarding workflows) that genuinely takes expertise to build. If packs are thin, the marketplace is thin.

What Is Being Sold

Deployment packs wrapped in commercial infrastructure. Not prompts, not raw agent configs. A marketplace asset is a complete package: parameterized template + onboarding workflow + integration guides + test scenarios + listing metadata + pricing + versioning. The buyer gets "fill form, get working agent" -- not "here's a config file, good luck."

Pricing Models for Assets

Sellers should have flexibility. GHL supports: free, one-time, monthly subscription, and yearly subscription. Third-party GHL snapshots sell for $97-$997 (one-time) on secondary markets. Trillet should support at minimum:

OPEN Asset pricing models (design decision)

  • Free -- seed marketplace, build reputation, attract buyers. Trillet still earns on per-minute usage from installations.
  • One-time -- single payment, permanent access. Seller captures upfront, Trillet takes cut + per-minute.
  • Subscription -- recurring payment, includes updates/support. Higher seller LTV, higher Trillet take over time.

What This Is Not

This is not a general app store. It is a vertical deployment pack exchange for voice AI agencies. The marketplace should resist scope creep toward plugins, integrations, or non-template assets. The unit of exchange is the deployment pack -- a complete, deployable, commercially wrapped voice agent template for a specific business vertical.

Design Rationale

The compressed logic behind this architecture. What changed from v1 and why.

Governing Principles

INFERENCE Derived from revenue data + competitive research + strategic frame

  1. Opinionation scales with repeatability. More repeatable = product decides more. D2C = maximally opinionated. Enterprise = minimally opinionated.
  2. Customization is allowed upward. Must not leak downward. Enterprise absorbs complexity. Agency gets defaults. D2C gets a fixed product.
  3. The template engine bridges regimes. Enterprise proves → templates productize → agencies distribute.
  4. Anti-generosity constraint. Every gate given away at Core is an upgrade that can't be sold at Scale. Generous where it doesn't cost upgrade pressure; strict where it does.

What Changed (v1 → v2)

Three decisions were revised based on competitive research across Stammer, Assistable, VoiceAIWrapper, Insighto, ChatDash, EchoWin, and GHL. Full research: market-research/competitor-feature-research/round3-consolidated.md

REVISED Workspace counts finalized: Starter 5 hard cap, Agency 10 + overage, Scale 15 + overage
History: Core cap revised 20 → 10 (Mar 11) based on market evidence: Assistable Growth ($450) gives 10, Stammer gives 20 at $197. Finalized (Mar 18): Starter 5 hard cap (testing tier, no overage), Agency 10 + $20/ea overage (growth-friendly), Scale 15 + $20/ea overage (generous for machine-builders).
Overage logic: Hard cap only at Starter (forces upgrade). Agency and Scale have overage to retain growing agencies rather than forcing churn.
RESOLVED Mar 11 Core billing: pass-through → full markup (all tiers)
Previous logic: Strictly pass-through at Core preserved markup as Scale's key commercial gate. Revised to "limited markup" after GPT-5.4 challenge, then further resolved to full markup after stress test.
Final resolution (Mar 11): Full markup at ALL agency tiers. No gating, no caps. Markup IS the agency business model. 4 of 6 competitors give full rebilling at entry tier. Per-minute revenue (2-4x subscription per agency) means gating markup optimizes the wrong variable. Premium brands include everything and charge for scale. The only gates are operating-mode transitions: sub-account count, automated self-signup, marketplace distribution.
Full reasoning: rebrand-vc/tier-architecture-stress-test.md
REVISED Template gate: single feature → five-object hierarchy
Previous logic: Template creation was gated to Scale. Only Scale could build reusable vertical packages.
New evidence: Template anatomy research (7 competitors mapped) revealed the market decomposes along three independent axes: completeness, parameterization, and distribution. No voice AI competitor covers all three. Competitive "templates" range from prompt presets (Stammer) to full agent clones (ChatDash) to account-level snapshots (GHL). The word conflates five distinct objects.
Resolution: Replace single "template" feature with an object hierarchy: prompt presets (all tiers) → agent blueprints (Entry+) → parameterized templates (Core+) → deployment packs (Entry consumes, Core+ creates) → marketplace assets (Scale). Each tier unlocks a qualitatively different object, making the upgrade a business model transition (consumer → creator → distributor), not just "more template features." The gate is the object level, not creation vs distribution.

Decisions That Hold From v1

LOCKED Unchanged by competitive research

  1. Agency ladder gates are commercial capability, not product features. Each step unlocks what the agency can DO commercially.
  2. Per-minute by tier: $0.12 at Starter and Agency; $0.10 at Scale (volume discount). Subscription increase is the primary upgrade price. The per-minute discount at Scale adds an additional incentive for high-volume agencies.
  3. Buyer-stage alignment, not feature gating. Tiers match where the buyer IS in their business.
  4. $497 for Scale. GHL parity. Commercially rational at the upgrade math.
  5. D2C is contained, not eliminated. Exists at $49/mo. Separate routing.
  6. Custom work is separated from product. Services don't distort packaging.
